Imagine this: you ask a traditional large language model, "What time is it?" It will guess, relying on patterns in its training data which is likely over a year out of date. It doesn't actually "know" the time. Now consider an AI agent. Not only can it check the system clock or call an API for the exact time, but it could also take the next step: recording the time in a spreadsheet, scheduling a meeting, or triggering a notification.

This distinction marks a major leap forward. While pure LLM based chatbots generate outputs based solely on their training, more agentic AI systems actively engage with external systems and perform multi-step tasks. For the insurance industry, this evolution presents a huge opportunity. Not quite a revolution yet, but a clear path toward reshaping processes, reducing manual effort, and enabling smarter workflows.

## RPA vs. AI Agents: What's New?

The insurance sector has relied heavily on Robotic Process Automation (RPA) to streamline repetitive tasks. RPA, while effective for structured and predictable workflows, often falls short when faced with inconsistencies like variable document formats or unstructured customer inputs.

AI agents offer something more: flexibility and adaptability. Unlike RPA's rigid scripts, AI agents can dynamically adjust to new data, changing formats, or unforeseen scenarios. For example:

- RPA might automate data entry from standardized claims forms, but if the format changes, it requires reprogramming.
- An AI agent, on the other hand, could recognize the updated structure, adapt, and continue processing without interruption.

This adaptability is crucial in insurance, where workflows often involve unstructured data from emails, customer documents, or phone transcripts.

## Potential Applications of AI Agents in Insurance

While AI agents are not yet widespread, their potential use cases in insurance are compelling:

### 1. Underwriting Support

Underwriting involves numerous manual steps, from reviewing broker submissions to entering data into quoting systems. An AI agent could:

- Extract key details from emails or PDFs.
- Verify the completeness of submissions.
- Populate the quoting system automatically.
- Draft follow-up emails for missing information.

This type of automation could reduce bottlenecks and free underwriters to focus on decision-making and risk assessment.

### 2. Claims Processing

AI agents could simplify claims workflows by:

- Validating submitted documents.
- Extracting key details like policy numbers and claim amounts.
- Triaging cases based on complexity or urgency.

Agents' ability to adapt to diverse inputs makes them well-suited to handle the variability that stymies RPA solutions.

### 3. Customer Service Assistance

Routine inquiries such as policy updates or payment status checks could be handled autonomously by AI agents. For more complex issues, the agent could gather relevant information before escalating to a human representative, creating a seamless hybrid service experience.

## Why AI Agents Matter for the Future of Insurance

AI agents aren't here to replace existing tools but to complement them. By integrating with established systems and APIs, they can amplify efficiency and accuracy without requiring companies to overhaul their tech stack. For example:

- Instead of rebuilding a fraud-detection system, an AI agent can work alongside it, using outputs to trigger follow-up actions or reports.
- When handling tasks like address validation, an AI agent can call proven APIs rather than trying to replicate functionality.

This combination of adaptability and integration makes AI agents particularly suited for industries like insurance, where reliability and precision are paramount.

## Challenges to Adoption

While the potential is clear, insurers face challenges in adopting AI agents, including:

- **Integration Complexity**: Legacy systems and siloed data can make it difficult to deploy advanced AI solutions.
- **Regulatory Compliance**: AI agents must operate within stringent regulatory frameworks to avoid legal or reputational risks.
- **Change Management**: Shifting workflows to include AI agents requires buy-in from employees and careful training.

## The Path Forward

AI agents represent a natural evolution of automation in insurance, but they are not a plug-and-play solution. Insurers will need to take deliberate steps to test, refine, and scale these systems in specific workflows.

Key areas to watch:

1. **Incremental Deployment**: Start with low-risk, high-volume tasks like email triage or document processing to build confidence and refine the technology.
2. **Regulatory Alignment**: AI agents must operate transparently and in compliance with insurance-specific regulations.
3. **Human-AI Collaboration**: Design workflows where AI agents handle repetitive tasks, while humans oversee decisions and complex scenarios.

The full-scale adoption of AI agents in insurance may still be on the horizon, but their potential to enhance efficiency, accuracy, and scalability is undeniable. Insurers shouldn't wait for a perfect, polished solution to emerge. Now is the time to act.

Start small, but start somewhere. Identify a safer sandbox or a low-risk application where you can experiment with these tools. Organizations that start early and learn fast will have a significant advantage over those who wait.
